# Sourdough Crumb Classifier: Training Data Framework
## Source: Open Crumb Mastery by Trevor J. Wilson

---

## 1. PRIMARY CRUMB CLASSIFICATION CATEGORIES (Labels)

Wilson defines 4 main crumb types + 1 modifier trait:

### 1.1 CLASSICAL ARTISAN CRUMB (Open Irregular)
**Core definition:** Varying mix of large and small alveoli with clusters of lighter or denser areas. The crumb appears to "flow" — it may show swirling tendencies, lean to one side, or have a few very large (quarter-sized) holes scattered throughout.

**Visual indicators:**
- High variance in hole size (mix of large, medium, small alveoli)
- Clustering: denser pockets alongside open pockets
- Directional flow or swirl in alveoli arrangement
- Occasional very large holes (quarter-sized or bigger)
- No uniformity — deliberately irregular
- Can still support a spread (jam/honey won't drip through)
- Not excessively extreme — "open but functional"

**Positive markers:** Irregular flow, size variation, organic feel
**Negative markers (would disqualify):** Uniformly even spacing, large dense gummy zones, tunneling under crust

---

### 1.2 HONEYCOMB CRUMB (Open Even)
**Core definition:** Open crumb with fairly uniform alveoli distribution throughout the slice. Not a perfect matrix, but the overall texture reads as even — moderate variation in hole size, little clustering.

**Visual indicators:**
- Consistent hole size throughout the slice (moderate variation)
- Even spatial distribution — no heavy clustering to one side
- Very light and airy appearance — "cross section of a cloud"
- Soft and billowy looking texture
- No dense zones
- Deep, regularly spaced holes ideal for catching butter
- When also "lacy": appears like actual honeycomb in a hive

**Positive markers:** Evenness throughout, no dense patches, uniformity without being perfectly geometric
**Negative markers:** Large outlier holes, dense clusters, irregular zones

---

### 1.3 MOLTEN CRUMB (Tartine-Style)
**Core definition:** Most extreme open crumb. Wild, unpredictable. Looks like lava rock — visible gluten strands locked in place during baking. Large glistening alveoli with possible mild tunneling. "Custardy" in eating quality.

**Visual indicators:**
- Large dominant alveoli that appear glossy/glistening
- Visible gluten strands stretched between holes
- Occasional dense cluster as an exception (mostly air)
- Mild tunneling may be present
- Elongated, warped, or "disturbed" cell shapes
- Structure appears to still be "flowing" even though baked
- High contrast between very open areas and rare denser zones

**Positive markers:** Gluten strand visibility, large glistening alveoli, evidence of extreme fermentation/hydration
**Negative markers:** Dense uniformity, small holes, tight crumb zones

**Context requirement:** Only achievable with very wet dough (more liquid than solid consistency)

---

### 1.4 FOOL'S CRUMB (Underfermented)
**Core definition:** A few large holes scattered in otherwise tight, dense, gummy crumb. Misleads new bakers. The large holes are surrounded by clumps of dense, under-developed crumb — not true open crumb.

**Visual indicators:**
- A few isolated large holes (sometimes a tunnel under the top crust)
- Surrounding crumb is tight, dense, and visually gummy/compact
- Heavy loaf relative to its volume
- Dense zones dominate the slice — open spots are isolated outliers
- Crumb appears "wet" or uncooked looking in dense zones
- Lacks overall airiness despite a couple large holes

**Positive markers (for classification):** Isolated large holes amidst predominantly tight/dense crumb
**Key distinguisher:** The RATIO — mostly dense with a few open spots (not the reverse)

**Root cause signature:** Product of underfermentation — gluten not fully developed, gas not properly distributed

---

### 1.5 LACY CRUMB (Modifier Trait, not standalone category)
**Core definition:** A trait that any of the first three categories can exhibit. Every hole appears inflated to maximum capacity. No dense spots whatsoever. Crumb glistens. Delicate webbing between alveoli.

**Visual indicators:**
- Every single cell appears maximally inflated
- Zero dense spots — it's "mostly just air"
- Glistening appearance — like a snowflake in sunlight
- Delicate, thin cell walls (web-like)
- Very thin gluten strands visible between holes
- Most commonly achieved via retarded (overnight refrigerator) proofing

**Applied as modifier to:**
- Lacy Classical Artisan (open, irregular, glistening)
- Lacy Honeycomb (most desirable — looks like actual honeycomb in a hive)
- Lacy Molten (extreme, glistening, fully open)

## 2. FERMENTATION LEVEL DESCRIPTORS

### 2.1 Underfermented / Fool's Crumb Zone
**Crumb outcome:** Tight, dense, gummy. A few isolated large holes (gas escaped unevenly). Heavy loaf.
**Visual crumb signs:**
- Predominantly tight/dense crumb throughout
- Isolated large bubbles or a single tunnel near top crust
- Gummy, paste-like appearance in dense zones
- No overall airiness
- Crumb appears underdone or raw in texture
- Compressed, clumping zones

**Loaf-level signs (before slicing):**
- Little oven spring
- Scored area may burst dramatically (uncontrolled explosion rather than ear)
- Heavy for its size
- May not dome well

---

### 2.2 Proper Fermentation / Open Crumb Zone
**Crumb outcome:** Evenly open, whether irregular or regular. Light and airy. Fully cooked through.
**Visual crumb signs:**
- Distributed airiness throughout the entire slice
- No large dense patches dominating
- Full oven spring evidence (tall, well-risen loaf)
- Alveoli are well-formed and baked into place
- Cell walls are thin but intact
- Rich, roasted color throughout crumb (deep baking from open structure)

**Loaf-level signs:**
- Good oven spring and bloom
- Score opens cleanly (ear formation)
- Loaf is tall and round, not spreading flat
- Sounds hollow when tapped on bottom
- Feels light for its size

---

### 2.3 Slightly Overproofed
**Crumb outcome:** Open but somewhat "limp" — structure lacks snap. Slightly reduced oven spring.
**Visual crumb signs:**
- May still be fairly open but holes look slightly deflated or irregular in a collapsed way
- Score didn't open as cleanly
- Loaf appears lower profile than expected
- Crumb may look compressed vertically (spreading rather than rising)

---

### 2.4 Severely Overproofed
**Crumb outcome:** Collapsed, flat, bubbly crust. Structure fallen in on itself.
**Visual crumb signs:**
- Dense but also oddly holey — collapsed and irregular simultaneously
- Loaf is flat and wide
- Crumb may show "falling" structure — holes compressed from above
- Top crust may blister or bubble
- Gummy, wet-looking interior even when fully baked

## 3. CRUMB REGULARITY SPECTRUM

Wilson describes crumb structure as falling along a **Gaussian/bell-curve distribution**:

```
[Dense Regular] → [Open Irregular] → [Open Regular/Honeycomb]
 (underfermented     (peak of bell          (extended bulk,
  or stiff dough)        curve)              well-organized)
```

**Dense Regular (left tail):**
- Many tiny, tightly packed cells
- Uniform but compact
- Heavy, little airiness
- Typical of underfermented or very stiff dough

**Open Irregular (bell curve peak — Classical Artisan):**
- Mix of large and small cells
- Cells have warped/coalesced unevenly
- Highest visual variety in hole size
- ~30–50% volume increase during bulk

**Open Regular (right tail — Honeycomb):**
- Large but evenly distributed cells
- Large outliers have "died off," medium cells dominate
- ~60–80% volume increase during bulk
- Less variance, more consistency

**Key insight:** At both ends of the spectrum the crumb LOOKS even, but for entirely different reasons — underfermented crumb is dense-even (small cells), well-fermented-even crumb is open-even (medium cells). The classifier must distinguish these.

## 4. HOLE MORPHOLOGY VOCABULARY

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Alveoli** | Individual gas pockets/holes in the crumb |
| **Cell walls** | Thin gluten strands separating holes |
| **Coalescence** | Two cells merging into one larger cell |
| **Tunneling** | Large elongated hole running horizontally, often near top crust |
| **Clustering** | Dense grouping of small cells forming a "zone" within otherwise open crumb |
| **Dense patch** | Area of compressed, small-celled crumb |
| **Glistening** | Glossy appearance on cell walls — sign of high hydration and good fermentation |
| **Webbing/lacy** | Extremely thin cell walls giving a delicate, spider-web appearance |
| **Elongated cells** | Cells stretched in one direction — common in molten crumb or disturbed dough |
| **Irregular clustering** | Uneven grouping of cells — denser zones vs. open zones |

## 5. VISUAL DIAGNOSTIC FRAMEWORK (Wilson's Assessment Method)

### 5.1 The Airiness vs. Openness Distinction
- **Airiness** = total volume of air in the loaf (light vs. heavy feel)
- **Openness** = presence of visible alveoli/holes of moderate to large size
- A loaf can be airy without being open (e.g., Wonder Bread — airy but no holes)
- **True open crumb requires BOTH**: light overall AND visible holes
- Dense crumb with a few large holes = NOT open (Fool's Crumb)

### 5.2 The 80/20 Diagnostic Rule
Wilson's primary framework: **80% of crumb outcome = fermentation + dough handling**
- Wet dough does NOT cause open crumb — this is a common misdiagnosis
- Big holes in dense crumb = handling/fermentation failure, not hydration success
- Wet dough amplifies the effects of fermentation/handling but doesn't substitute for them

### 5.3 Crumb Structure Causality Chain
For diagnosing WHY a crumb looks a certain way:

1. **Gas accumulation** → primary driver of structure (more gas = more open)
2. **Fermentation level** → controls how much gas accumulates and when
3. **Folding regime** → redistributes gas, increases regularity
4. **Dough handling intensity** → more handling = more even, smaller, denser cells
5. **Hydration** → amplifier of all above (wet dough = more extreme outcomes)
6. **Retarding** → allows maximum gas accumulation → lacy crumb potential

### 5.4 Cell Size Distribution as Primary Classifier Signal
- **High variance (large + small mixed)** → Classical Artisan / Irregular
- **Low variance (medium-only)** → Honeycomb / Even
- **Extreme high variance (very large + sparse)** → Molten
- **Low variance + tiny cells** → Underfermented / Dense
- **Very large + no dense patches + glistening** → Lacy modifier present

### 5.5 Loaf-Level Visual Signals (Before Slicing)
| Signal | Likely Crumb Outcome |
|--------|---------------------|
| Tall, domed, good bloom | Proper fermentation → open crumb possible |
| Large ear (score opened well) | Good oven spring → proper proof |
| Flat and spreading | Underproofed or overproofed — dense crumb likely |
| Dramatic burst through crust | Underproofed — Fool's Crumb likely |
| Score barely opened | Overproofed — limp/compressed crumb |
| Flat top, blistered crust | Severely overproofed — collapsed structure |
| Loaf feels light for its size | Open crumb present |
| Loaf feels heavy for its size | Dense crumb |

## 6. CRUMB ANALYSIS PATTERNS FROM CASE STUDIES

### Stiff Dough (~65% hydration) — Classical Artisan
- Very light, open, lacy crumb achieved
- Stiff dough appears tight and dense by default — fermentation MUST reach full potential
- Handle very gently (dough's lack of extensibility invites rough handling — resist this)
- Finger poke test unreliable for stiff dough — inherently tense even when underproofed

### Medium-Soft Dough (~70% hydration) — Honeycomb-leaning
- Even, airy crumb throughout
- Strong, well-structured dough → drives regularity
- Openness from good fermentation; evenness from dough strength
- Wilson's preferred "daily bread" crumb

### Soft Dough (~75% hydration) — Open, lacy
- Very open and lacy
- "More like a cross section of a cloud than a loaf of bread"
- Extensibility of dough allows large cells to form and hold

### Wet Dough (~85% hydration) — Molten
- Extremely open, irregular, lacy, glistening
- Visible gluten strands; elongated/disturbed cells visible
- Can see "scar" where gluten snapped
- Achieved via overnight bulk retard + overnight final proof retard

### Wet Dough (~85% hydration) — Honeycomb
- Same recipe as above but stiffer effective feel (fresh-milled flour absorbs more water)
- More organized, even structure
- Result of fully doubled volume rise during retarded bulk

### Very Wet Dough (Ciabatta) — Molten to Extreme
- Most irregular possible
- Very wide variety of cell sizes scattered throughout
- Long fermentation + minimal handling

## 8. EDGE CASES AND DISAMBIGUATION NOTES

- **Fool's Crumb vs. Classical Artisan:** Key distinguisher is the RATIO of open to dense. Fool's Crumb is mostly dense with a few isolated open spots. Classical Artisan is mostly open with some variance.

- **Honeycomb vs. Dense Closed:** Both read as "even" — distinguish by absolute cell size and loaf weight. Honeycomb cells are large and regularly spaced; dense crumb cells are tiny and packed.

- **Molten vs. Classical Artisan:** Molten has visible gluten strands, glistening, and extreme cell sizes. Classical Artisan is irregular but not extreme — still functional for eating.

- **Lacy Honeycomb vs. Lacy Molten:** In lacy honeycomb, cells are even-sized and maximally inflated with delicate walls. In lacy molten, cells are large and irregularly sized but also maximally inflated.

- **High hydration ≠ open crumb:** Wet dough creates the illusion of openness through isolated large holes in otherwise dense crumb. True openness requires OVERALL airiness — the whole slice must be light.

- **Retarded vs. room-temp proofed:** Retarded loaves more commonly achieve lacy crumb due to cold gas contraction allowing greater gas accumulation per cell. Room-temp proofed loaves can still be open but lacy is rarer.

## SOURCE A: The Sourdough Journey — "How to Read a Sourdough Crumb" (PDF)

**Author:** Tom Cucuzza, The Sourdough Journey

### A.1 Five-Level Proofing Classification Table

This is the most granular publicly available proofing classification scheme. Five discrete levels assessed across six visual dimensions:

| Dimension | Significantly Underproofed | Slightly Underproofed | Nicely Proofed | Slightly Overproofed | Significantly Overproofed |
|-----------|---------------------------|----------------------|----------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **Loaf Height** | Flat | Tall and pyramidal, sometimes with a "belly" on bloom | Tall with high shoulders and "bunny" profile | Past peak height, falling shoulders | Flat |
| **Alveoli / Holes** | Dense, gummy, spongy crumb with some tunnels | Dense areas with tunnels or large holes | Even distribution of small, medium and large holes | Mostly medium to small holes | Smaller holes throughout with ragged edges |
| **Crumb Profile** | Dense strip at bottom, tunnels or holes on top | Dense strip at bottom, "fool's crumb" or large holes near top | Consistent distribution of holes edge to edge and top to bottom | Consistent distribution of small to medium holes throughout | Small holes throughout with separation of crust from crumb |
| **Ear / Ovenspring** | No ear, no ovenspring | Sometimes a very tall ear and tall ovenspring | Tall, pronounced ear with tall ovenspring | Smaller ear with less ovenspring | No ear and minimal ovenspring |
| **Bloom (scoring line)** | Does not open | Large "belly" bulging under the bloom | Gluten strands visible across bloom | Fewer strands and more bubbles visible in bloom | "Bubbles" visible and irregular-edged bloom |
| **Crust Color** | Pale, blonde crust | More blonde than brown | Multiple shades of light to dark brown | Multiple shades of light to dark brown | Blonde or dark, mottled crust |

### A.2 Per-Level Detailed Visual Descriptors

**Significantly Underproofed:**
- Flat overall shape, low ovenspring
- Dense, gummy, spongy crumb
- Dense clusters of small holes
- Tunneling near top of loaf, penetrating through loaf
- No ear
- Compact loaf with shiny, blonde crust
- Root cause: weak/immature starter, or too short/cold bulk fermentation

**Slightly Underproofed:**
- Taller, pyramidal shaped loaf
- Dense crumb at bottom
- Large holes or tunnels, usually near top
- Uneven distribution of holes
- "Fool's crumb" — irregular large holes on top half of loaf
- Tall ear with gas bubble under the bloom
- More blonde than brown crust
- Root cause: very common for beginning bakers; bulk fermentation needs more time

**Nicely Proofed:**
- Tall, airy loaf with high shoulders and "bunny" profile
- Consistent distribution of small, medium, and large holes
- No areas of dense, gummy crumb
- Fully proofed top to bottom and side to side
- Tall, pronounced ear
- Gluten strands visible across bloom
- Multiple shades of light to dark brown crust

**Slightly Overproofed:**
- Flattening loaf with falling shoulders
- Past peak height
- Higher density of small to medium holes; larger holes near crust
- Small or no ear
- Separation of crust from crumb, thinning crust
- Fewer gluten strands and more bubbles visible across bloom
- Holes become more ragged and irregular in shape as overproofing progresses

**Significantly Overproofed:**
- Flat loaf with minimal ovenspring and no ear
- Tighter crumb with some density at bottom
- Smaller holes throughout, more ragged-edged holes vs. round holes
- Deterioration of crumb; starting to separate from crust
- Gluten deterioration visible on ear (ragged edge)
- Bubbles visible in bloom
- Slack, irregular, asymmetrical shaped loaf
- Root cause: overnight bulk at too warm a temperature; allowing dough to double in warm conditions

### A.3 Gallery Label Taxonomy (image label set)
The PDF's gallery uses these as distinct image categories:
- `badly_underproofed` — Dense, gummy crumb
- `underproofed` — Dense areas and giant holes
- `underproofed` — Dense crumb, tunneling
- `underproofed` — Spongy crumb and caverns
- `slightly_underproofed` — Pyramidal shape with tunneling
- `nicely_proofed_closed` — Slightly closed crumb
- `nicely_proofed_open_irregular` — Open, irregular crumb
- `nicely_proofed_molten` — Molten crumb
- `nicely_proofed_wild` — Wild crumb

- `slightly_overproofed` — Nice crumb but losing shape
- `significantly_overproofed` — Dense crumb and flat shape

### A.4 Key Diagnostic Insight
Slightly underproofed loaves can exhibit a very tall ear and significant ovenspring (more than a properly proofed loaf in some cases) because the dense, under-expanded dough has a lot of energy left that erupts in the oven. This is a common misread: tall ear ≠ well-proofed. Context of the crumb profile is required.

## SOURCE B: The Sourdough Journey — Over/Under Proofed FAQ
**URL:** https://thesourdoughjourney.com/faq-over-under-proofed/

### B.1 Flour & Hydration as Hard Ceilings on Crumb Openness

| Factor | Crumb Effect |
|--------|-------------|
| Low-protein flour (all-purpose) | More closed crumb regardless of technique |
| Hydration below 70% | Generally closed crumb |
| High-protein bread flour (12%+) | Open crumb potential increased |
| Hydration 75–78% | More open crumb potential |
| Whole wheat / heirloom grains | Naturally produce closed crumbs |

**Classifier implication:** An image that looks like a tight crumb may be correctly fermented for its flour type. Flour type context matters for labeling.

### B.2 Common Misdiagnosis Warning
Reducing cold retard time has almost no impact on fermentation quality — adjust bulk fermentation instead. Many bakers over-attribute crumb problems to cold retard when the real variable is bulk fermentation duration.

### B.3 Proofing Level Parallels Cooking Doneness
The FAQ uses the analogy: underproofed = underdone, overproofed = overdone, properly proofed = just right. This reinforces that there is a continuous spectrum, not discrete states.

## SOURCE C: Challenger Breadware — Identifying Proofing Levels in Baked Bread
**URL:** https://challengerbreadware.com/bread-techniques/identifying-proofing-levels-in-baked-bread/

### C.1 Three-Level Classification with Exterior + Interior Indicators

**Under Proofed:**
- Exterior: Uncontrolled tearing in the sides (excess CO2 escaping after crust formation), considerable height but uneven overall shape, poor or absent ear development, smaller width and length
- Interior (crumb): Tight and gummy, dense composition with uneven air bubble distribution

**Properly Proofed:**
- Exterior: Even expansion in length and width, shape retention during baking, good oven spring with beautiful ear development, beautiful brown crackly caramelized crust
- Interior (crumb): Consistent structure throughout, soft and fluffy, larger and evenly dispersed air bubbles

**Over Proofed:**
- Exterior: Very flat loaf with minimal rise, loss of shaping definition, destroyed structural integrity preventing shape retention, pale or light-colored crust (depleted sugars)
- Interior (crumb): "Very open crumb structure" from excess CO2 development — paradoxically, overproofed crumb can appear open but the loaf is flat and structurally failed

### C.2 Crust Color as Fermentation Proxy
Fermentation consumes the sugars needed for crust caramelization (Maillard reaction):
- **Underproofed:** Pale/blonde (sugars still present but crust formed before caramelization completed from oven burst)
- **Properly proofed:** Rich multi-tone brown (ideal sugar consumption + bake)
- **Overproofed:** Pale or mottled (sugars depleted, poor caramelization)

**Classifier implication:** Crust color is a secondary but meaningful diagnostic signal when crumb image also includes crust context.

### C.3 Shape and Dimensions as Quickest Assessment
Shape and size provide the fastest evaluation of proofing accuracy — interior crumb confirms the exterior read.

## SOURCE D: The Perfect Loaf — Ultimate Guide to Bulk Fermentation
**URL:** https://www.theperfectloaf.com/guides/the-ultimate-guide-to-bread-dough-bulk-fermentation/

### D.1 Four-Cue Holistic Assessment Framework
The Perfect Loaf (Maurizio Leo) argues against any single metric and proposes assessing four cues together:

1. **Elasticity & Strength** — Dough surface texture and resistance to stretching; progresses from shaggy to smooth with defined edges
2. **Visual Rise & Structure** — Domed edges, puffiness; the dough should appear "alive and airy" (this is described as secondary to feel/elasticity)
3. **Temperature Monitoring** — Final Dough Temperature (FDT) controls fermentation speed; warmer (~80–82°F) = faster bulk completion; cooler = extended time
4. **Intuitive/Experience-based judgment** — Accounting for flour type, hydration, and levain ripeness

### D.2 Critical Warning: Volume Increase Is Misleading
"Judging when to end bulk fermentation based on how much the dough rises (volumetric increase) can be misleading." The state of the dough matters more than a numerical rise percentage.

### D.3 Properly Fermented Bulk Dough Indicators
- Elastic, strong dough with defined edges
- Resists stretching (elasticity present)
- Puffy and jiggles when shaken gently
- Rounded, domed edges
- Appears alive and airy

## SOURCE E: The Perfect Loaf — How to Bake Open Crumb Sourdough
**URL:** https://www.theperfectloaf.com/how-to-bake-open-crumb-sourdough-bread/

### E.1 Open Crumb Definition
"A loaf that's light in hand and evenly (and appropriately) open in the interior — one whose light texture contributes to the overall eating experience."
- Ideal: "clear, defined holes consistently dispersed throughout"
- NOT ideal: scattered large cavities mixed with dense spots

### E.2 False Open Crumb Visual Markers (Underproofed Masquerading as Open)
- Scattered large holes with strange, snaking, or squished shapes
- Dense spots alongside aerated regions
- Excessive upward rise with dome-shaped bottom
- Erratic rupturing despite proper scoring
- Dull, uninteresting crust
- Flat flavor profile

### E.3 Final Proof Readiness Indicators
- Dough risen "75% to 80% of its full height" (leaving reserve for oven spring)
- Feels "alive, puffy, light, yielding but not weak"
- Poke test: impression slowly springs back (not immediately, not staying depressed)

### E.4 Six-Factor Framework for Open Crumb Achievement
1. Strong white flour (11–13% protein)
2. Ample fermentation with properly timed starter
3. Higher hydration (70–80% range)
4. Appropriate dough strengthening
5. Batard (oval) shaping preferred over boule
6. Precise final proof timing

### E.5 Poke Test Calibration
- Under-proofed: impression springs back immediately (too much tension)
- Properly proofed: impression slowly springs back completely or almost completely
- Over-proofed: indentation remains; soft, weak feel; may deflate

## SOURCE F: The Bread Code — Debugging Your Crumb
**URL:** https://www.the-bread-code.io/tutorial/2022/04/14/debugging-your-crumb.html

### F.1 Root-Cause Crumb Classification

| Crumb Problem | Visual Indicator | Root Cause |
|--------------|-----------------|------------|
| **Underfermented** | "Super pockets of air" where water evaporates, resembling unleavened pita structure; large irregular isolated holes | Short fermentation; insufficient gas production |
| **Perfect** | Even distribution of pockets of air | Optimal fermentation timing |
| **Overfermented** | Dough cannot maintain structure; stays flat; bacteria degrades gluten | Excessive fermentation time |
| **Underbaked** | Very gummy sections throughout | Insufficient water evaporation during bake |
| **Too hot** | Alveoli near crust converge into "mega pockets" | Rapid crust formation; avoid above 230°C/446°F |
| **Insufficient steam** | Same outcome as too hot — merged air pockets near crust | Crust sealed too quickly, preventing proper expansion |

### F.2 Key Diagnostic Distinctions
- **Overfermented ≠ open crumb:** Overfermented loaves go flat because gluten is broken down. A "very open" appearance in an overproofed loaf is actually structural failure — the holes are from collapsed gluten, not from well-formed alveoli.
- **Gummy crumb:** Can be caused by bacterial gluten breakdown (overfermentation) OR by underbaking. Distinguish by loaf shape — overfermented loaf is flat; underbaked loaf may have good shape.
- **Mega pockets near crust vs. interior:** Large holes clustered near the crust (not throughout) suggest oven temperature or steam problem, not fermentation issue.

### F.3 Multi-Variable Nature of Crumb
Key variables affecting crumb outcome beyond fermentation:
- Oven temperature and steam management
- Water content
- Flour type (whole wheat ferments faster — accelerated gluten degradation risk)

## SOURCE G: Trevor Jay Wilson — Underfermentation Instagram Recap
**URL:** https://trevorjaywilson.com/underfermentation-instagram-recap-6/

### G.1 Structural Root Cause Framework (Bricks & Mortar Analogy)
- Gas accumulation = "bricks" (provides internal structure)
- Gluten network = "mortar" (holds the structure together)
- Underfermentation = insufficient bricks → gluten (mortar) alone cannot support the loaf
- External support from a banneton cannot compensate for lack of internal structural integrity

### G.2 Physical Manifestations of Underfermentation

**Dough behavior:**
- Excessive spreading and flattening (pancaking) after shaping
- Loss of surface tension post-shaping
- Sticking severely to banneton despite flour application
- Minimal oven spring and poor ear development

**Loaf characteristics:**
- Flat, sloped appearance lacking height
- Dense, heavy crumb
- Gummy interior despite adequate baking

**Crumb:**
- Fool's Crumb pattern — a few large holes/tunnels surrounded by dense, gummy crumb
- Appears deceptively open due to scattered large voids
- Lacks overall airiness; heavy, custardy texture

### G.3 Underfermentation Diagnostic Steps
1. Evaluate starter vigor: vigorous starters triple within 6 hours at room temperature
2. Monitor bulk rise: target 30–50% volume increase for adequate gas production
3. Assess fermentation progress through objective volume measurement, not subjective signs alone

### G.4 Three-Part Causality Chain
1. Healthy starter → healthy fermentation
2. Healthy fermentation (gas production) → solid dough structure
3. Solid dough structure → loaf stands tall and holds shape

## SOURCE I: Pantry Mama — Underfermented Sourdough
**URL:** https://pantrymama.com/underfermentedsourdough/

### I.1 Five Visual Indicators of Underfermented Sourdough

1. **Scoring issues:** Cracked scoring and large, over-pronounced ears with breaks outside intended score lines
2. **Crumb structure:** Uneven crumb — tight crumb surrounding random larger holes rather than uniform texture
3. **Moisture content:** Gummy, wet texture that persists even after cooling; knife leaves residue marks on interior
4. **Crust development:** Bread won't brown in oven and won't brown in toaster — due to insufficient residual sugars consumed during fermentation
5. **Overall appearance:** Loaf is small and feels heavy, with inadequate oven spring

### I.2 Root Cause Description
Underfermented bread retains excess energy in the yeast. Insufficient time during bulk fermentation prevents proper gas development and the sugar production needed for browning reactions. The crumb fails to fully cook regardless of internal temperature readings.

### I.3 Paradox: Tall Ear on Underfermented Loaf
Underfermented dough with a lot of residual yeast energy can produce a very tall, dramatic ear because the dough still has substantial expansion energy. This is NOT a sign of good fermentation — it is a sign of excess unreleased energy. The crumb will be tight and gummy despite the dramatic exterior.

### I.4 The Gummy Knife Test
A practical diagnostic tool for cut loaves: draw a knife through the crumb — if the blade picks up doughy residue, the bread is either underfermented or underbaked. A properly fermented and baked crumb releases cleanly from the knife.

## SOURCE K: Simply Bread — How to Read Your Crumb Like a Seasoned Baker
**URL:** https://www.simply-bread.co/post/how-to-read-your-crumb-like-a-seasoned-baker

### K.1 Three Primary Diagnostic Dimensions

1. **Alveoli (gas pockets):** Size, shape, and distribution reveal fermentation quality
   - Well-developed: varied sizes, evenly dispersed, supported by gluten network
   - Problematic: extremely large pockets/tunnels, overly uniform, or collapsed appearance

2. **Loaf shape (exterior read before slicing):**
   - Properly fermented: tall silhouette, strong shoulders, clean bloom with upward lift
   - Under-fermented: flat/pancake shape, shallow score, lacks opening
   - Over-fermented: sunken shoulders, collapse after scoring, minimal bloom

3. **Crumb texture:** Assessing strength and absence of gumminess

### K.2 Under-Fermented Visual Characteristics
- Alveoli are "compressed rather than developed"
- Dense areas between holes with insufficient gas production
- Irregular bubble formation; potential shaping bubbles on surface
- "Shaped too early, before enough fermentation had occurred"
- Tunneling patterns suggesting incomplete bulk rise

### K.3 Over-Fermented Visual Characteristics
- Bubbles have "expanded past their peak and lost tension"
- Slack, stretched appearance with collapsed cell walls
- "Fragile, custard-like feel" with inward slumping
- Gelatinized interior, glossy texture
- Crust separation, deflated shoulders, minimal score bloom

### K.4 Well-Fermented Visual Characteristics
- "Mix of small and medium alveoli that are evenly distributed"
- Thin, slightly glossy cell walls indicating strong development
- Clear vertical lift with balanced top-to-bottom structure
- Thin cell walls and no signs of collapse or thick tunnels
- Gentle, elastic interior

### K.5 Contextual Interpretation Warning
Flour type, starter maintenance, methodology, and timing all influence interpretation. A tight crumb in a whole-grain loaf may be correct; a tight crumb in a high-protein white flour loaf signals a problem. Visual classification always requires contextual metadata.

## PART 3: CROSS-SOURCE SYNTHESIS

### Consensus Visual Indicators by Proofing Level

The following synthesizes indicators that appear across multiple independent sources:

#### Significantly Underproofed (Consensus)
- Flat loaf, low height
- Pale/blonde crust (insufficient sugar consumption)
- No ear or very minimal ear
- Dense strip at bottom of crumb slice
- Some tunneling near top
- Heavy for size
- Gummy crumb even after cooling
- Knife picks up dough residue

#### Slightly Underproofed / Fool's Crumb Zone (Consensus)
- Pyramidal or asymmetric loaf shape
- Tall ear possible (paradox — excess unreleased energy)
- Dense crumb at bottom half, large isolated holes in top half
- Uneven hole distribution — dense zones dominate
- Crumb does not brown well in toaster
- Knife residue present

#### Properly Proofed (Consensus)
- Tall loaf with high, even shoulders ("bunny" profile)
- Multi-toned brown crust (light to dark)
- Pronounced ear with gluten strands visible in bloom
- Even distribution of small, medium, and large holes throughout
- No dense zones — fully proofed top-to-bottom and edge-to-edge
- Loaf feels light for its size
- Crumb releases cleanly from knife
- Poke test: slow, complete spring-back

#### Slightly Overproofed (Consensus)
- Falling/sunken shoulders
- Smaller ear, less ovenspring
- Crust beginning to separate from crumb
- Holes become smaller and more ragged-edged
- Fewer gluten strands in bloom, more bubbles
- Less crust browning

#### Significantly Overproofed (Consensus)
- Flat loaf, minimal height
- No ear
- Pale or mottled crust (sugars depleted)
- Small, ragged-edged holes throughout
- Crumb separation from crust
- Slack, asymmetrical shape
- Gelatinized or glossy interior

### Cross-Source Disambiguation Additions

- **Tall ear ≠ well-proofed** (Pantry Mama, Sourdough Journey): A very tall ear can indicate significant underproofing where excess yeast energy bursts through the score. Must read crumb interior — tall ear + dense gummy crumb = underproofed.

- **"Open" overproofed crumb** (Challenger Breadware, Bread Code): Overproofed bread can appear to have very open, large holes — but this is structural failure/collapse, not true open crumb. The loaf will be flat, the crust pale, and the structure will have no spring. Distinguish from true open crumb by loaf height and crust color.

- **Isolated large holes ≠ open crumb** (Wilson, King Arthur, Perfect Loaf, Pantry Mama): Multiple independent sources confirm that scattered large holes in otherwise dense crumb are NOT a sign of well-fermented open crumb. They are a consistent marker of underfermentation (Fool's Crumb).

- **Fermentation window is narrow** (Pump Street chart): The difference between optimal fermentation and over-fermentation can be as little as 1–2 hours in a controlled protocol. This reinforces the importance of fine-grained classification.

- **Whole grain accelerates overfermentation** (Bread Code, King Arthur): Whole wheat and other whole-grain flours have increased enzymatic activity that degrades gluten faster. A loaf that looks overproofed at the same time as a white flour loaf may be correctly labeled as overproofed rather than well-fermented.
